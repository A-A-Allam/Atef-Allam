Biomechanics: How Physical Forces Shape Healing and Fibrosis

Visual Elements:

  • Left: Artery/fibroblast under mechanical stress (animated arrows).
  • Right: Graph of "Stress-Strain Curve" in healthy vs. fibrotic tissue.

Key Points:

Definition:

Biomechanics = Study of how forces (tension, shear, stiffness) affect living tissues.

Key Players:

  • ECM Stiffness: Fibrotic tissue is 10x stiffer than normal.
  • Cellular Sensors: Integrins, YAP/TAZ, mechanosensitive ion channels.

Disease Links:

  • Fibrosis: Stiff matrix → Activates myofibroblasts → More collagen.
  • Atherosclerosis: Shear stress alters endothelial cell behavior.

Speaker Notes

"Biomechanics isn't just about physics—it's a language cells use to communicate. Here's how it works:

  • In a healthy heart, flexible ECM allows normal contraction. But after infarction, stiff scar tissue forces cardiomyocytes to work harder, leading to failure.
  • Fibroblasts sense stiffness via integrins, activating YAP/TAZ—a pro-fibrotic switch. This creates a vicious cycle: fibrosis begets more fibrosis.
  • In blood vessels, low shear stress (e.g., at bifurcations) promotes atherosclerosis by triggering endothelial inflammation.

Clinical Example:

  • Liver stiffness (measured by FibroScan) predicts cirrhosis progression.
  • Therapeutic angle: Drugs targeting YAP/TAZ (e.g., verteporfin) are in trials for fibrosis.

Optional Add-On Slides

How to Measure Biomechanics:

Atomic force microscopy, traction force microscopy.

Therapeutic Targets:

LOXL2 inhibitors (e.g., simtuzumab) for ECM crosslinking.

Shear Stress and Atherosclerosis: Key Mechanisms

1. Low Shear Stress (LSS) Promotes Atherosclerosis

Location:

Occurs at arterial bifurcations (e.g., carotid sinus, coronary bifurcations) and inner curvatures (e.g., aortic arch).

Mechanisms:

Endothelial Dysfunction:
  • ↓ eNOS activity → Reduced NO production → Vasoconstriction, inflammation.
  • ↑ ROS (NADPH oxidase-driven) → Oxidative stress.
Pro-Inflammatory Signaling:
  • ↑ NF-κB → Increases adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1) → Monocyte recruitment.
  • ↑ TLR4/MyD88 pathway → Promotes cytokine release (IL-6, TNF-α).
Plaque-Prone Phenotype:
  • ↑ LDL retention (due to disturbed flow).
  • ↑ Macrophage infiltration → Foam cell formation.

2. High Shear Stress (HSS) is Protective

Location:

Found in straight arterial segments (e.g., mid-left anterior descending artery).

Mechanisms:

Endothelial Quiescence:
  • ↑ eNOS/NO → Anti-inflammatory, vasodilatory.
  • ↑ Kruppel-like factor 2 (KLF2) → Suppresses NF-κB.
Anti-Atherogenic Effects:
  • ↓ LDL retention (laminar flow clears lipids).
  • ↑ Autophagy → Clears damaged organelles.

3. Clinical Evidence

Imaging Studies:

  • 4D Flow MRI shows plaque develops in low-shear regions.
  • OCT/IVUS: Thin-cap fibroatheromas (vulnerable plaques) correlate with LSS.

Animal Models:

ApoE⁻/⁻ mice with induced LSS develop plaques at bifurcations.

4. Therapeutic Implications

Drugs Targeting Shear-Sensitive Pathways:

  • Statins ↑ eNOS/NO.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin) ↑ KLF2.

Endothelial Mechanomodulation:

  • Stent design to restore laminar flow.
  • Exercise ↑ shear stress → Improves endothelial function.

5. Summary

Shear Stress Type Effect on Endothelium Atherosclerosis Risk
Low (Disturbed) Inflammatory, Dysfunctional High (Plaque formation)
High (Laminar) Protective, Quiescent Low (Plaque resistance)

Key Takeaway

  • Low shear stress drives atherosclerosis via inflammation, LDL retention, and endothelial dysfunction.
  • High shear stress is protective but can erode plaques if flow is turbulent.
