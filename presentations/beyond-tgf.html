Beyond TGF-β/MMP: Additional Factors Driving Fibrosis and Chronic Wounds

The development of fibrosis or chronic non-healing wounds is not solely dependent on dysregulated TGF-β/MMP signaling. Other critical factors include:

1. Persistence and Density of the Fibrin Network

Fibrin's Role in Early Inflammation:

  • Forms a provisional matrix for cell migration (neutrophils, macrophages).
  • Acts as a scaffold for fibroblast infiltration and ECM deposition.

Pathological Implications:

  • Excessive/Uncleared Fibrin → Chronic fibrosis (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis, keloids).
  • Fibrinolytic Deficiency (↓ Plasminogen/TPA activity) → Impaired fibrin degradation → Persistent inflammation.
  • Fibrin Crosslinking by Factor XIIIa → Increased mechanical stiffness → Fibroblast activation.

2. Microbial Load & Biofilm Formation

Bacterial Persistence:

  • Biofilms (e.g., S. aureus, P. aeruginosa) resist phagocytosis → Sustained M1 polarization → Chronic inflammation.
  • Endotoxin (LPS) → TLR4 activation → Pro-fibrotic cytokine release (IL-1β, TNF-α).

Viral/Fungal Infections:

  • Hepatitis C → Liver fibrosis via oxidative stress.
  • Chronic Candida → Non-healing ulcers.

3. Impaired Engulfment of Fibrin & Cellular Debris

Defective Efferocytosis:

Macrophage Dysfunction (e.g., in diabetes) → Failure to clear apoptotic cells → Secondary necrosis → Pro-inflammatory DAMPs (HMGB1, ATP).

"Fibrin Traps":

Fibrin can entrap inflammatory cells (NETs, dead neutrophils) → Forms a pro-inflammatory nidus.

If not resolved, it evolves into hyalinized scar tissue.

4. Hypoxia & Oxidative Stress

Ischemia-Reperfusion Injury:

ROS → ECM crosslinking → Stiff matrix → Myofibroblast activation.

HIF-1α Stabilization:

Drives VEGF (angiogenesis) but also fibrosis in chronic hypoxia (e.g., diabetic ulcers).

5. Biomechanical Forces

Tissue Stiffness:

Fibroblasts sense matrix rigidity via integrin-ROCK pathway → Sustained α-SMA expression → Fibrosis.

Shear Stress in Blood Vessels:

Altered flow → Endothelial dysfunction → TGF-β release.

6. Does the Fibrin Network Persist? Why?

Yes, fibrin can persist due to:

  • Defective Fibrinolysis (↓ plasminogen activation).
  • Inhibition by PAI-1 (overexpressed in diabetes/obesity).

Macrophage Failure to Engulf:

Fibrin is not phagocytosed like cells; it requires plasmin-mediated dissolution.

If plasmin is inhibited (e.g., by α2-antiplasmin), fibrin persists.

7. Therapeutic Implications

Target Approach Example
Fibrinolysis Recombinant tPA Alteplase for chronic wounds
Biofilm Antibiotic-loaded hydrogels Vancomycin-eluting dressings
Efferocytosis Annexin A1 mimetics Resolvin E1 trials
Oxidative Stress Nrf2 activators Bardoxolone methyl

8. Key Takeaways

  • Fibrin density, infection, hypoxia, and macrophage dysfunction are independent drivers of fibrosis/chronic wounds.
  • Uncleared fibrin acts as a pro-inflammatory scaffold, worsening fibrosis.
  • Biofilms and mechanical stress perpetuate non-healing states.
  • Therapies must combine fibrinolysis, infection control, and macrophage reprogramming.
